## Script

**[Hook, varies each episode. 0:00 to 0:20]**

Every organisation is drowning in voices. The loudest person in the meeting. The dashboard with forty numbers on it. The story everyone repeats until it starts to sound like a fact. We call all of that information. Most of it is noise. The hard part, the part nobody really trains you for, is telling the one signal that matters from all the static around it.

**[Opener tag, fixed. 0:20 to 0:35]**

I'm Nelson Jatel, and this is The Signal, where we trace the weak ties behind how decisions get made and how change really happens.

**[The noise, how we perceive it. 0:35 to 1:25]**

Here is how the noise gets us. We look at the org chart and assume the boxes near the top are where decisions happen. We measure people one at a time, their titles, their opinions, their output, and we treat the loudest as the most important. And when something stalls or goes sideways, we hunt for a person to blame instead of a pattern to understand. It feels rigorous. Often it is just confident noise: a tidy story, told with certainty, that points us in the wrong direction.

**[The instrument: social networks and weak ties. 1:25 to 2:25]**

Social network analysis asks a different question. Instead of measuring people one at a time, you measure the relationships between them. And the moment you do, a hidden structure appears. The org chart is not the network. The person who actually holds things together is often quiet, an unglamorous connector who links two groups that would otherwise never talk. [On screen: a single amber line traced out of a field of nodes.] Those connectors, those bridges, are what this series is named for. The sociologist Mark Granovetter called them weak ties, and showed that these loose, bridging connections carry more new information than our closest relationships do. Weak ties are how information, and decisions, and change actually travel.

**[Why it matters: decisions and change. 2:25 to 3:10]**

This is not academic. It changes two things everyone cares about. First, decisions. If you can see who is really connected to whom, you can see who actually drives a decision, not who is supposed to. Second, change. Change does not move through memos or mandates. It moves through relationships, across those weak-tie bridges. So if you can map them, you can see where a change will spread and where it will quietly die. [Water and policy are one place I have watched this play out, but it is just as true in any team or any organisation.]

**[The project and the lessons. 3:10 to 4:10]**

My own way into this was a fifty-year study of a single network. Five decades of who talked to whom, how the relationships shifted, and what that did to the decisions that came out the other end. Three things stuck with me. First, the people the system treated as central often were not the ones holding it together. [Add one concrete, surprising example from the study, kept general rather than naming the organisation.] Second, the change was the story, not the snapshot. The network only revealed itself when you watched it move over years, not in any single moment. And third, [your single sharpest lesson, one sentence]. None of that was visible on an org chart. It was only visible once we traced the ties.

**[Sign-off, three beats, fixed shape. 4:10 to 4:40]**

So that is the work here: take the noise of a real decision or a real change, and trace the one signal worth acting on. Next time, [tease the next episode in one line]. If that is useful to you, subscribe, and read the longer version in The Signal.

It all feels like noise, until you trace the signal.
