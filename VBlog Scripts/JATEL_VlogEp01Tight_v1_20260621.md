## Script

**[Hook. 0:00 to 0:15]**

Every organisation is drowning in voices. The loudest person in the room. The dashboard with forty numbers on it. The story everyone repeats until it sounds like a fact. We call it information. Most of it is noise.

**[Opener tag, fixed. 0:15 to 0:30]**

I'm Nelson Jatel, and this is The Signal, where we trace the weak ties behind how decisions get made and how change really happens.

**[The idea. 0:30 to 1:30]**

Here is the trap. We read the org chart and assume the boxes near the top are where decisions happen. We measure people one at a time, and treat the loudest as the most important. It feels rigorous. Usually it is just confident noise. Social network analysis asks a different question: instead of measuring people one at a time, measure the relationships between them. Do that, and a hidden structure appears. The org chart is not the network. The person who actually holds things together is often quiet, a connector who links two groups that would otherwise never talk. [On screen: a single amber line traced out of a field of nodes.] Those bridges are what this series is named for. Mark Granovetter called them weak ties, and showed they carry more new information than our closest relationships do. Weak ties are how information, decisions, and change actually travel.

**[Why it matters. 1:30 to 2:10]**

That changes two things. Decisions: see who is really connected, and you see who actually drives a call, not who is supposed to. Change: it does not move through memos, it moves across those weak-tie bridges. So map them, and you can tell where a change will spread and where it will quietly die.

**[The proof. 2:10 to 2:40]**

I learned this the slow way, in a fifty-year study of a single network. The lesson that stuck: [your one sharpest finding, in a sentence, kept general]. None of it showed up on an org chart. It only showed up once we traced the ties.

**[Sign-off. 2:40 to 3:00]**

So that is the work here: take the noise of a real decision or a real change, and trace the one signal worth acting on. Next time, [tease the next episode in one line]. Subscribe, and read the longer version in The Signal.

It all feels like noise, until you trace the signal.
