## Speaking

**Eyebrow (mono):** KEYNOTES · WORKSHOPS · BOARD SESSIONS

**H1:** The room already has a network. I help you see it.

**Intro (first person):**
Every board, every leadership team, every conference room runs on relationships
that no org chart shows. I use social network analysis to make those relationships
visible: who actually decides, where influence concentrates, and where a network is
brittle enough to fail under pressure. You leave with a clearer picture of how your
organisation really works, and a few specific things to do about it.

### Keynote topics

**What Does Your Network Actually Look Like?**
The hidden map of influence inside any board or organisation, and why the official
structure rarely matches it.

**Tracing the Signal.**
How to find what matters in a noisy organisation, and how to tell a real pattern
from a story you wanted to be true. Ties directly to the video series.

**The Hidden Architecture of Water Governance.**
The distinctive niche: social network analysis applied to who actually decides when
water, land, and competing interests meet. Drawn from a fifty-year study of one
region's governance network.

**Weak Ties, Strong Decisions.**
Why the informal connections between people, the ones no policy accounts for, so
often drive or stall the formal outcome.

- Keynote (30 to 60 minutes)
- Half-day workshop
- Board or leadership working session

### What you get

Not just a talk. Depending on format, you can leave with a map of your own decision
network, a read on where influence is concentrated, and a short list of the
relationships worth strengthening before they are tested.

## About

**Eyebrow (mono):** WATER · NETWORKS

**H1:** I study how decisions really move through an organisation.

**Story (first person):**
I came to networks through water. For more than two decades I worked inside water
governance in British Columbia, the meetings, the boards, the long negotiations
between people who did not always agree on what the water was for. Over time I
noticed that the formal record, the agendas and the minutes, explained almost
nothing about how decisions actually happened. The real story was in the
relationships.

So I went and measured them. My doctoral research applied social network analysis
to a fifty-year record of regional water-governance meetings, mapping how influence
and decision-making moved through one institution across half a century. What it
showed, again and again, is that the people an organisation thinks are central
often are not, and the connections that hold everything together are usually the
ones nobody is watching.

That is the work I bring to other rooms now. The patterns are already there, in
water, in governance, and in any organisation that runs on relationships. The job
is making them visible, so leaders can act on what is real instead of what is
assumed.
